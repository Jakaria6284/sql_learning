Easy: Build an Electronics Inventory Report
Select, filter, and sort your first real query
📋 Business Context: The inventory manager needs a quick list of all electronics in stock, sorted by price to identify which items are most and least expensive. This helps with pricing decisions and stock warnings.
🎯 Your Task:
Write a query that shows:
• Product name
• Unit price
• Stock quantity

Only for Electronics category, with most expensive items first.
💡 Hint: Use SELECT to pick columns, WHERE to filter category, and ORDER BY DESC to sort by price highest-first.
✓ Expected result format: About 15-20 electronics rows with prices ranging $50-$500+, most expensive at top.
Solution (Click to reveal)
SELECT product_name, unit_price, stock_qty
FROM products
WHERE category = 'Electronics'
ORDER BY unit_price DESC;

Why This Approach Works

  1. WHERE category = 'Electronics' — Filters to only electronics before sorting (faster than sorting all products).
  2. ORDER BY unit_price DESC — Descending order shows highest prices first, perfect for pricing strategy.
  3. SELECT product_name, unit_price, stock_qty — Exact columns needed; no wasted data.

Business Insight

If stock_qty is low for high-price items, it's a restock priority. If stock_qty is high for low-price items, you might run a sale to move inventory.

Medium: Find Loyal Customers
JOIN + GROUP BY + HAVING combined
📋 Business Context: The customer retention team wants to identify "loyal" customers—those who've made multiple purchases. These VIPs deserve special attention, discounts, and personalized marketing.
🎯 Your Task:
Write a query that shows:
• Customer name
• Total number of orders they've placed

Only show customers with MORE THAN ONE order, sorted by order count (highest first).
💡 Hint: You need to:
1. JOIN customers to orders (to link names with orders)
2. GROUP BY customer to count orders per person
3. HAVING to filter for count > 1 (not WHERE)
4. ORDER BY count descending
✓ Expected result format: 8-12 customer names with order counts (2, 3, 4+). No one-time customers.
Solution (Click to reveal)
SELECT c.full_name, COUNT(o.order_id) AS order_count
FROM customers c
JOIN orders o ON c.customer_id = o.customer_id
GROUP BY c.customer_id, c.full_name
HAVING COUNT(o.order_id) > 1
ORDER BY order_count DESC;

Why This Approach Works

  1. JOIN customers c ... orders o — Links customer names to their orders.
  2. GROUP BY c.customer_id, c.full_name — Bunches all orders per customer into one row.
  3. HAVING COUNT(o.order_id) > 1 — HAVING (not WHERE) because we're filtering AFTER grouping. WHERE filters before.
  4. ORDER BY order_count DESC — Top customers (most orders) first.

Key Insight: WHERE vs HAVING

  • ❌ Wrong: WHERE COUNT(...) > 1 → Doesn't work; WHERE can't use aggregates.
  • ✅ Right: HAVING COUNT(...) > 1 → Filters groups after aggregation.

Business Insight

These customers (2+ orders) are your retention gold mine. A 10% discount to keep them costs less than acquiring new customers.

Hard: Department Budget Analysis
CTEs, JOINs, CASE logic, and calculations combined
📋 Business Context: The CFO needs to review each department's budget health. Some departments blow their budgets (overstaff, high salaries), others underspend (vacancies, low-paid staff). The finance team uses this report monthly to approve raises, hiring freeze, or reallocation.
🎯 Your Task:
Write a query that shows FOR EACH DEPARTMENT:
• Department name
• Employee count (headcount)
• Total payroll (sum of salaries)
• Budget amount (from departments table)
• Remaining budget (budget - payroll)
• Budget status: "OVER BUDGET" (payroll > 105% of budget), "UNDER BUDGET" (payroll < 95% of budget), or "ON TARGET"

Sort by remaining budget (smallest first—the most over-budget departments appear first).
💡 Hint: You need to:
1. Calculate payroll stats per department ONCE (use WITH/CTE)
2. JOIN that CTE to departments table
3. Calculate remaining = budget - payroll
4. Use CASE to categorize status (check ±5% margins)
5. ORDER BY remaining
✓ Expected result format: 3-4 departments with budget comparisons. Some over (negative remaining), some under (large remaining).
Solution (Click to reveal)
WITH payroll AS (
  SELECT department_id, 
         COUNT(*) AS headcount, 
         SUM(salary) AS total_payroll
  FROM employees
  GROUP BY department_id
)
SELECT d.dept_name, 
       p.headcount, 
       p.total_payroll, 
       d.budget,
       d.budget - p.total_payroll AS remaining,
       CASE
         WHEN p.total_payroll > d.budget * 1.05 THEN 'OVER BUDGET'
         WHEN p.total_payroll < d.budget * 0.95 THEN 'UNDER BUDGET'
         ELSE 'ON TARGET'
       END AS budget_status
FROM departments d
LEFT JOIN payroll p ON d.department_id = p.department_id
ORDER BY remaining;

Why This Approach Works

  1. WITH payroll AS (...): A CTE (Common Table Expression) that calculates headcount and payroll per department ONCE. Cleaner than subqueries and easier to read.
  2. LEFT JOIN payroll: LEFT JOIN keeps all departments, even if they have no employees (rare but safe).
  3. CASE logic: Applies a ±5% tolerance:
    • If payroll > budget × 1.05 → OVER (exceeds budget by >5%)
    • If payroll < budget × 0.95 → UNDER (uses <95% of budget)
    • Otherwise → ON TARGET (within 5%)
  4. ORDER BY remaining: Negative remaining first = departments most over budget (finance priority).

Advanced Concept: CTE (WITH)

A CTE is a "named temporary query" that you define once and reuse. It makes complex queries readable and prevents calculating the same thing multiple times.

Business Insight

If a department is OVER BUDGET by 10-15%, the CFO might freeze hiring or cap bonuses. If UNDER BUDGET, they might approve equipment purchases or raises to stay on track.

Expert: Sales Performance by Category
Real-world analytics: JOINs, GROUP BY, aggregates, sorting
📋 Business Context: The sales director reviews quarterly performance by product category. They want to see which categories generate the most revenue, how many items sold, and average price point. This data drives inventory planning and marketing budget allocation.
🎯 Your Task:
Write a query that shows FOR EACH PRODUCT CATEGORY:
• Category name
• Total items sold (sum of order quantities)
• Total revenue (sum of quantity × unit_price)
• Average item price
• Number of unique products in category

Sort by total revenue highest-first.
💡 Hint: You need to:
1. JOIN products → order_items (links categories to sales)
2. GROUP BY category
3. SUM(quantity), SUM(quantity * price), AVG(price), COUNT(DISTINCT product_id)
4. ORDER BY revenue DESC
✓ Expected result format: 4 categories with revenue totals, likely ranging $5K-$50K each.
Solution (Click to reveal)
SELECT 
  p.category,
  SUM(oi.quantity) AS items_sold,
  SUM(oi.quantity * p.unit_price) AS total_revenue,
  ROUND(AVG(p.unit_price), 2) AS avg_price,
  COUNT(DISTINCT p.product_id) AS num_products
FROM products p
JOIN order_items oi ON p.product_id = oi.product_id
GROUP BY p.category
ORDER BY total_revenue DESC;

Why This Approach Works

  1. SUM(oi.quantity) — Total items sold across all orders in category.
  2. SUM(oi.quantity * p.unit_price) — Revenue per item × quantity = total revenue (not in DB, calculated on fly).
  3. AVG(p.unit_price) — Average price point (useful for positioning: "budget" vs "premium" category).
  4. COUNT(DISTINCT p.product_id) — How many unique products per category (inventory breadth).
  5. ROUND(avg_price, 2) — Rounds to 2 decimals for nice currency display.

Business Insight

If Electronics generates 60% of revenue but only 20% of items sold, they're high-margin. If Furniture is 40% of items but 20% of revenue, it's low-margin and may need repricing.

Challenge: Product Review Sentiment
CASE WHEN with text categorization
📋 Business Context: The product team wants to categorize customer reviews by sentiment (positive, neutral, negative) based on review score. Products with too many negative reviews should be investigated (quality issues?) or discontinued.
🎯 Your Task:
Write a query that shows FOR EACH PRODUCT:
• Product name
• Number of reviews
• Average review score
• Sentiment category based on average score:
- Score ≥ 4.5: "Excellent"
- Score ≥ 3.5 and < 4.5: "Good"
- Score ≥ 2.5 and < 3.5: "Average"
- Score < 2.5: "Poor"

Sort by average score descending (best-rated first).
💡 Hint: You need to:
1. JOIN products to reviews
2. GROUP BY product_id, product_name
3. COUNT(review_id) and AVG(review_score)
4. Use CASE to categorize by score thresholds
5. ORDER BY avg_score DESC
Solution (Click to reveal)
SELECT 
  p.product_name,
  COUNT(r.review_id) AS num_reviews,
  ROUND(AVG(r.review_score), 2) AS avg_score,
  CASE
    WHEN AVG(r.review_score) >= 4.5 THEN 'Excellent'
    WHEN AVG(r.review_score) >= 3.5 THEN 'Good'
    WHEN AVG(r.review_score) >= 2.5 THEN 'Average'
    ELSE 'Poor'
  END AS sentiment
FROM products p
LEFT JOIN reviews r ON p.product_id = r.product_id
GROUP BY p.product_id, p.product_name
ORDER BY avg_score DESC;

Why This Approach Works

  1. LEFT JOIN reviews — LEFT JOIN keeps products even if no reviews exist (new products).
  2. CASE WHEN AVG(r.review_score) >= 4.5 — Checks aggregated average, not individual scores.
  3. ROUND(AVG(...), 2) — Rounds to 2 decimals for readability.
  4. ORDER BY avg_score DESC — Highest-rated products first for quick identification of winners.

Why LEFT JOIN?

If you use INNER JOIN and a product has no reviews yet, it won't show up. LEFT JOIN shows all products, and you'll see NULL / 0 for review count on new items.

Business Insight

Products with "Poor" sentiment need investigation: Is it a quality issue? Shipping damage? Wrong expectations? Poor reviews hurt sales, so prioritize fixes here.

Quick Reference: Common Patterns
Cheat sheet for tests and interviews
Output Specific Columns: SELECT full_name, salary FROM employees;
Filter Rows: WHERE category = 'Electronics' AND price > 100;
Sort Results: ORDER BY salary DESC LIMIT 10; (top 10 paid employees)
Remove Duplicates: SELECT DISTINCT country FROM customers;
Count / Sum / Average: SELECT COUNT(*), SUM(salary), AVG(salary) FROM employees;
Group and Filter: GROUP BY dept HAVING COUNT(*) > 5; (departments with >5 employees)
Link Two Tables: FROM employees e JOIN departments d ON e.dept_id = d.dept_id;
Conditional Output: CASE WHEN salary > 100000 THEN 'Senior' ELSE 'Junior' END AS level;
Temporary Query (CTE): WITH high_earners AS (SELECT * FROM employees WHERE salary > 80000) SELECT * FROM high_earners;
🔑 Pro Tip: When building complex queries, test them piece by piece. Start with one table (SELECT + WHERE), add JOINs next, then GROUP BY + HAVING, then ORDER BY. Don't try to write it all at once!
